How Much Will You Have for Retirement in 2026?

This free calculator projects your retirement savings using compound growth. It factors in your current savings, age, contributions (employee + employer match), expected returns, and inflation. As of 2026, the average employee contributes about 9.5% of salary to their 401(k), with employers adding ~4.7% on average.

Key Assumptions & 2026 Context

  • Average total contribution rate (employee + match) is around 14%
  • Historical stock/bond portfolio returns average ~7% after inflation in many projections
  • Safe withdrawal rate is approximately 3.9% for a 30-year retirement (Morningstar 2026 guidance)

Practical Tips to Reach Your Goals

Increase Savings Faster

  • Maximize employer match — it's free money
  • Increase contributions by 1% each year
  • Consider catch-up contributions if age 50+
  • Automate increases with every raise

Protect Your Nest Egg

  • Diversify investments and rebalance regularly
  • Keep fees low (target under 0.5%)
  • Plan for healthcare and long-term care costs
  • Factor in Social Security and pensions

Frequently Asked Questions

How is the future value calculated?

Using the compound interest formula with annual contributions added each year. It accounts for your current savings growing plus new contributions growing over time.

What is the safe withdrawal rate?

The 3.9% rule (2026 Morningstar guidance) means you can withdraw about 3.9% of your portfolio in the first year of retirement, adjusted for inflation each year, with a high probability of the money lasting 30 years.

Should I include employer match?

Yes — it's essentially free money. The average match in 2026 is around 4.7% of salary. Make sure you're contributing enough to get the full match.

Are these numbers guaranteed?

No. Returns, inflation, and contribution amounts can vary. This is an educational projection only. Past performance does not guarantee future results.

How can I improve my retirement outlook?

Increase your contribution percentage, start earlier, reduce investment fees, or delay retirement by a few years. Even small consistent increases compound powerfully.
